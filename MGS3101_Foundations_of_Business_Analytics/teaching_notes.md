# Teaching Notes – MGS 3101 Foundations of Business Analytics  
## Fall 2025 W03

## Course Development Background

This course was substantially redesigned and redeveloped for Fall 2025 **at the request of the Program Director**.

At the time of preparation, no prior instructional materials from previous offerings of MGS 3101 were available for reuse. Existing syllabi from Kean University and Wenzhou-Kean University provided only limited guidance regarding scope and topic coverage. As a result, **the course was developed substantially from the ground up**.

---

## Course Design Considerations

### Benchmarking and External References

In redesigning the course, I referenced several external and internal materials to benchmark appropriate scope, rigor, and positioning, including:

- BT1101 Introduction to Business Analytics (National University of Singapore)
- Existing MGS 3101 syllabi from Kean University and Wenzhou-Kean University

---

### Curriculum Alignment within BA Program

A major design objective was to position MGS 3101 appropriately within the Business Analytics curriculum.

The course was designed to:

- Build upon students’ prior preparation in:
  - MGS 2150 – Business Statistics
  - MGS 3001 – Python Programming for Business

- Prepare students for subsequent advanced BA coursework:
  - MGS 3501 – Database Management in Business
  - MGS 3701 – Data Mining in Business
  - MGS 4701 – Integrative Applications of Business Analytics

Accordingly, MGS 3101 was structured as a transitional bridge course connecting foundational quantitative/programming preparation with more advanced analytical and technical BA coursework.

---

### Industry Relevance

To improve practical relevance and ensure alignment with market expectations, I consulted with professional contacts working in data science and analytics roles regarding skills commonly expected in industry.

Insights from these discussions informed the inclusion/emphasis of topics such as:

- Data cleaning and preprocessing
- Exploratory data analysis
- Data visualization for business communication
- Basic predictive modeling interpretation
- Translating analytical outputs into managerial insights
- End-to-end analytics workflow awareness

---

### Integration of Personal Research Practice

The course design also incorporates my own empirical research and analytical workflow practices, particularly:

- Use of Python for data analysis
- Jupyter Notebook for integrated coding, analysis, and presentation
- Reproducible analytical workflows commonly used in research and practice

This was intended to expose students to authentic analytics workflows beyond simplified textbook examples.

---

## Pedagogical Design Philosophy

The course was designed with the following instructional principles:

- Emphasize applied business analytics rather than purely technical/statistical theory
- Integrate technical execution with managerial interpretation
- Use hands-on demonstrations and practical examples extensively
- Prepare students both for advanced coursework and for industry-relevant analytics tasks

---

## Reflection and Future Improvements

### Strengths Observed
- Students responded positively to practical demonstrations and applied examples
- Jupyter Notebook workflow improved engagement and transparency of analysis process
- Course effectively bridged programming/statistics foundations and advanced BA coursework

### Planned Improvements
- Further refine pacing of technical modules for mixed-skill cohorts
- Expand real-world business case examples
- Improve scaffolding for students with weaker Python/statistics background
